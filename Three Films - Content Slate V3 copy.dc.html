Film 1 · ~30s series · On-camera desk + real footage · IG / TikTok / LinkedIn / Shorts

Play-by-Play

Real incident footage, treated with the reverence of a sports broadcast: one desk, one analyst, one telestrator. Two graphic devices only — the hand-drawn telestrator and the final scorebug that always reads NOTHING HAPPENED · FINAL.

The human truth

Every yard has tape like this. The camera saw everything and helped nothing.

Everyone in operations has watched a screwup on a security recording after the fact, when the only thing left to do is wince. The fail is the entertainment. Birdseye is the fifteen seconds of relief at the end.

How it lands in the feed

A frozen security-cam frame, a hand-drawn yellow circle, and a man at a desk saying “Alright, let’s go to the tape.” Your brain files it as sports before it files it as an ad, so you stay. The play is a guy strolling through an open gate, and the desk treats it like a blown coverage in the red zone. Then the same play runs against Birdseye, a voice comes over a speaker, and the intruder simply leaves. The scorebug posts the final: NOTHING HAPPENED. You send it to your ops guy: “this is your yard.”

Film 2 · ~27s · One set, one actor, cinematic chaos · IG / TikTok / Shorts

The Booth (Truckyard Doug)

Doug is played with love: calm, diligent, excellent at exactly the wrong scale, while the yard behind his window goes to pieces. The comedy targets the booth, never the man. Seven frames, 27 seconds.

The human truth

It was never a people problem. One person cannot watch forty acres.

Everyone has been Doug: handed an impossible job and coping through routine. Birdseye arrives not as Doug’s replacement but as the thing that should have had his back all along.

How it lands in the feed

It opens on a man watering a desk plant with a bottle cap, and behind him, soft and out of focus, someone is halfway over the fence. “Quiet one today.” He pencils in TRESPASS while one occurs directly behind his head. The gate is in two pieces; “Gate’s fine.” Then, with real pride, he flips the sign to DAYS WITHOUT INCIDENT: 400 while the window glows faint orange through drifting smoke, and a single crow calls. Hard cut to footage so calm it feels like another planet. After the logo: Doug, lawn chair, lake, fishing rod. “Doug’s fine, by the way.”

Film 3 · The flagship · ~12:00 documentary, chaptered for splicing · White-label Mantella

Nothing Happened

Working title; it is also the last line of the film. Three acts, six chapters, every chapter a standalone cut. Documentary craft throughout: a host who walks, real people who work, one night-time scene at the center that plays like a 90-second thriller.

The human truth

The most expensive thing at a yard is what everyone has stopped noticing.

And in security, the happy ending is that nothing happens. The chapter nobody else ever films — implementation — is where this film earns trust no testimonial can buy.

In the feed, the sales deck, and the trade show booth

It opens on a black screen and a real voice: “You are being monitored. Leave the property now.” A caption: Recorded at 2:47 AM. Then a man walking toward a gate at dawn says, “Most security stories start after something went wrong. This one starts before.” For twelve minutes you tour a real yard’s whole journey: the clipboard years, the costs nobody counted, the decision, the build, the go-live nerves, and one full night, told in real time, where the system catches what the old yard never saw. The film ends where it began, at the gate: “Nothing. Nothing happened.”

For production

Documentary honesty is the premium: the yard never performs, interviews sound like people, Ricky talks like a builder. The light does the storytelling, dawn to 2:47 AM to morning.

For marketing

White-label is a feature, not a constraint: “a mid-size trucking yard, somewhere in Ontario” means every viewer is allowed to see their own yard in it.

For leadership

The title is the thesis is the last line. When the host says “Nothing. Nothing happened,” the audience finishes the sentence with him — the moment people quote in meetings.
